## Notes

### Multi-Dimensional Vectorisation: Motivation

After bag-of-words/TF-IDF (Lecture 9.1), the lecture introduces a second family of vectorisation techniques: **word embeddings**, via two models — the **Skip-gram model** and **CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)**.

We want to represent that "cat" and "dog" are *related* — something bag-of-words/TF-IDF vectors cannot do, since every term is just an independent dimension. **Word embeddings** map semantically similar words to **nearby points** (projections on an n-dimensional plane), based on the **distributional hypothesis**: words that appear in the same context share semantic meaning.

### CBOW vs. Skip-gram

- **CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)** — predicts the **target word** from its **context words**.
- **Skip-gram** — does the inverse: predicts **context words** from the **target word**.

### Skip-gram — Worked Example

For *"the quick brown fox jumped over the lazy dog"*, using a **window** of words to the left and right of a focus word:

- **Focus word**: `quick`
- **Window size 1** → **context words**: `the` and `brown`
- **Whole dataset** (generated by sliding the window across the sentence): `(quick, the), (quick, brown), (brown, quick), (brown, fox), ...`
- The learning problem becomes: **predict (input, output) pairs** — i.e. search for semantically (or otherwise) related words, the *context* for a given word.

(The lecture also illustrates this with a **window size 2** example and diagrams of the matrix-as-lookup-table — see [[Word Embeddings (Word2Vec and GloVe)]] for the conceptual write-up.)

### From Trained Network to Embedding

**Train a NN on this (input, output) data and use the weights vector as the lookup** — the same principle applies to CBOW, just in reverse (predicting the target from context instead of context from target).

This makes Word2Vec an **unsupervised learning algorithm** for word vector representation — but it **does not use the NN in a conventional sense**: rather than caring about the network's predictions, training **uses the learned weights as the embedding matrix**, which then functions as a **lookup table** mapping each word to its dense vector.

### GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation

**GloVe** was developed by **Stanford** as an **open-source project**, trained on **Wikipedia**. Stanford provides pre-trained models at **25, 50, 100, 200, and 300 dimensions**, based on corpora of **2, 6, 42, and 840 billion tokens** respectively.

## Summary

This second half of Lecture 9 introduces **word embeddings** as a richer alternative to bag-of-words/TF-IDF: dense vectors where semantically similar words sit close together, motivated by the **distributional hypothesis**. **CBOW** and **Skip-gram** are inverse formulations — predict target-from-context vs. context-from-target — illustrated with a worked Skip-gram example on "the quick brown fox jumped over the lazy dog" (window size 1, focus word "quick" → pairs like `(quick, the)`, `(quick, brown)`). The key insight is that the **trained network's weight matrix becomes the embedding/lookup table** — the network's predictions themselves are discarded. **GloVe** (Stanford, trained on Wikipedia at 25-300 dimensions over 2-840 billion tokens) is introduced as a widely-used pre-trained alternative built on global co-occurrence statistics. Full conceptual treatment, pitfalls (static vs. contextual embeddings), and Python usage are in [[Word Embeddings (Word2Vec and GloVe)]]. Word embeddings are the input representation that the next lecture's [[Transformer (Attention Is All You Need)|Transformer architecture]] builds on.
